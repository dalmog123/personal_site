Enforcing IRC §871(m) on Dividend Equivalents

How the U.S. tried to tax synthetic dividends - and why, fifteen years and six deferrals later, the rules still can’t be enforced.

Almog Dror

You’re holding the paper. This page is a 3-minute orientation before you begin - every underlined term is one tap away from a plain-English definition.

The paper in 30 seconds

The short version

Foreign investors used total-return swaps to synthetically own U.S. stocks and dodge the 30% tax normally withheld on dividends. In 2010, Congress enacted §871(m) to tax these dividend equivalents as if they were the real thing. The regulation turns that goal into dense math - the Delta Test and the Substantial Equivalence Test.

The paper’s thesis: the law is conceptually sound but operationally unenforceable. The market engineers instruments that sit just under the thresholds, and the withholding agents lack the data to comply - which is why the IRS has deferred full enforcement six times, now to 2027. It closes with an original fix: the Tiered Withholding Simplification Model.

Why this exists

The situation, in three moves

The loophole

Under the old sourcing rules, payments on a notional principal contract were sourced to the recipient’s residence. So dividend equivalents on a swap escaped the 30% tax entirely, turning taxable U.S. income into tax-free foreign-source gains.

The mechanics

A foreign Long Party enters a total-return swap with a U.S. broker - the Short Party - who holds the physical shares. The dividend is paid domestic-to-domestic (untaxed), then its economic value is passed to the foreign investor through the swap. No withholding.

The response

The HIRE Act of 2010 applied substance over form through §871(m), and Treas. Reg. 1.871-15 (finalized 2015) operationalized it with the Delta and Substantial Equivalence tests.

A decade of deferrals

Fifteen years, still not enforced

  1. 2010HIRE Act enacts §871(m).
  2. 2015Final regulations (1.871-15) introduce the Delta and Substantial Equivalence tests.
  3. 2016Notice 2016-76 limits enforcement to delta-one transactions from 2017.
  4. 2017-22Notices 2017-42, 2018-5, 2018-72, 2020-2, 2022-37 push the horizon out, again and again.
  5. 2024Notice 2024-44 extends relief through 2026; non-delta-one and combination rules deferred to Jan 1, 2027.
  6. 2027The enforcement cliff - or a seventh deferral.

Before you read

Eight terms that unlock the paper

FDAP income

Fixed, Determinable, Annual or Periodical U.S.-source income - taxed at a flat 30% for foreign investors. Dividends are the archetype.

Dividend equivalent

A synthetic payment on a derivative that references a U.S. dividend. §871(m) recharacterizes it as taxable U.S.-source income.

Delta & the Delta Test

Delta is how much a derivative moves when the stock moves $1. A delta of 0.80+ makes it “specified” and taxable - but it’s tested only on day one.

Substantial Equivalence Test

For complex, non-linear contracts: a variance-based comparison against a hypothetical 0.80-delta benchmark.

Combination Rule

Aggregates separate trades entered “in connection with” each other, so an investor can’t split one exposure into sub-threshold pieces.

QDD

Qualified Derivatives Dealer - a status letting a firm net its positions and absorb withholding, so tax doesn’t cascade through a chain of trades.

Qualified Index

A safe harbor: derivatives on broad, diversified indexes are exempt if the index passes a strict seven-part test.

Cashless withholding

Tax owed on a “deemed” dividend when no cash actually changes hands - collected by debiting the client’s collateral or margin.

The core argument

What the paper actually claims

The revenue is a rounding error

A proposed expansion was scored by the JCT at just ~$90M over ten years, and Treasury has never published regime-wide collections - against a systemic, recurring compliance cost borne by every intermediary.

The precision is an illusion

A bright-line 0.80 threshold, tested only on day one, invites a purpose-built “0.79 delta” instrument. The deepest avoidance needs no dishonest inputs at all - it’s designed into the contract itself.

It can’t be policed

The Combination Rule presupposes cross-custodial portfolio visibility that clearing brokers in anonymous, exchange-traded markets simply do not have.

The proposed fix

The Tiered Withholding Simplification Model

Replace the 0% / 30% cliff with graduated bands, and eliminate the combination rule for listed securities:

delta < 0.400%
0.40 - 0.7910%
delta ≥ 0.8030%

“A lower rate that’s actually collected beats a 30% rate that isn’t.”
